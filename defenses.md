# PhD Milestones

The department guidelines for the qualifier exam (pg 24-25), preliminary exam (pg 25-26), research defense (pg 26) and final defense (pg 26-27) are available [here](https://virginiatech-my.sharepoint.com/:w:/g/personal/sara83_vt_edu/IQBCGrtZhLDMQoxWMrsyjNm4AVHtRJhMOeAiQftLwhGvH6I?e=M28AtA). More details specifically for our research group are provided below:

## Qualifier Exam

The goal of the qualifier exam is to show you are capable of doing research. The format of the exam will differ depending on the research area and who is on the committee, however all exams will require a written and oral component. You will typically sign up at the end of the fall semester, complete for the exam in January-February, and submit the deliverables in March. A sample qualifier exam format for software engineering can be found [here](https://chbrown13.github.io/qualifier.html). For this exam, your goal it to show you have the ability to:

* find and understand existing literature in your field;
* convey scholarly topics (via writing and oral presentation) to an academic audience; and
* identify gaps in existing research and propose a research direction to fill this gap (this may or may not be what you actually work on for your PhD).

## Preliminary Exam

The preliminary examination (a.k.a. dissertation proposal) will establish the expectations for you to complete your dissertation. It consists of a written document and oral presentation, both outlining your preliminary findings and proposing the final study/ies to complete your PhD. Before scheduling your preliminary exam, you will need to have some research completed (ideally published) and you must have your dissertation committee finalized, including your external committee member. Somewhere on the CS@VT website it was mentioned that your written document should be short (~15 pages) to just propose your final study, however most students use this as a first pass at your final thesis and way to start making early progress on dissertation writing---which I would also suggest. For the prelim exam, your goals should be to:

(a) motivate your committee that your research problem is a problem, it's a problem worth exploring, and you have an idea for a proposed solution to this problem;

(b) demonstrate how the completed work highlights the problem and/or motivates your proposed solution;

(c) present the technical details of your proposed studies for the remainder of your PhD;

(d) obtain feedback from your committee on the scope, direction and feasibility of your proposed work;

(d) provide a timeline for the completion of your PhD (this may change, but should provide an estimated time to complete the remaining milestones); and

(e) form an agreement between the you and the committee regarding what needs to be completed before you can graduate. 

**Presentation Style**: Technical: Provide details on the problem, methodologies, and the proposed work to provide solutions to your research problem. Be ready to explain your decisions and respond to questions/discussions.

## Research Defense

The goal of the research defense is "to ensure that students are adequately prepared for the Final Dissertation Defense" by identifying any concerns that may prevent a successful final defense. For students working with Dr. Brown, this should primarily focus on the technical aspects of your research. The goal should be to provide: 

(a) motivation for your work and the problem you are exploring; 

(b) a justification for how your work is novel and advances the field; 

(c) an (brief) overview the preliminary study methodologies and results from the completed work; and 

(d) an update on the methodologies, tool designs, and results so far from the propsed work. 

There is no written portion to this exam, only an oral presentation. The presentation should last no more than one hour, and be organized in a way so that you can receive constructive feedback to refine your work. Please be open to suggestions and critiques, as your committee will ultimately decide whether you pass the final dissertation defense. Every student should complete a research defense, unless there is an extenuating circumstance (i.e., you have a job that starts immediately).

**Presentation Style**: Technical: Provide details on methodologies, findings, and implications. Be ready to explain your decisions and respond to questions/discussions.

## Final Defense

The final dissertation defense is the last step to obtaining a PhD. While it may feel like the most daunting, it should be viewed as a celebration of your accomplishments and the work you completed (the research defense should be the more intimidating one). The dissertation will contain the techical details and in-depth results that the committee should be aware of through reading your dissertation. (Please also remember to check your references and revise the text of the final dissertation if your preliminary exam document was copy-pasted versions of previous papers!). Instead of being heavily technical and methodologically-focused, your final defense presentation should provide: 

(a) insights into the problem and motivation (clearly define the problem you set out to solve and why it is important);

(b) a justification for how your work is novel and advances the field, along with a thesis statement;

(c) highlights of your findings, only focusing on the key results that relate to your thesis statement; and

(d) the main takeaways from your work and the implications of your findings for future research or applications.

You must schedule your exam through the graduate school. Two weeks before the scheduled date, you must share a near-final version of the dissertation draft with your committee (the guidelines say three week, but I have always seen and heard two). The presentation should last no more than one hour, and be organized in a way to highlight your accomplishments! 

**Presentation Style:** Celebratory: Engaging for your audience and can simplify technical language (i.e., present as if you were describing your research to your parents). Some students also include slides for acknowledgments, etc. to highlight support from friends, family, etc.

## Presentation Quality

For all exams, you should exhibit high-quality presentation skills that demonstrate you are **the _expert_** in this field (especially for the research and final defenses). Some tips are below, but please also see the ["How to Give A Bad Talk 10 Commandments"](https://courses.washington.edu/b572/public/RiediBadTalk.pdf)

1. Use clear and direct language
  * Aim for straightforward language that conveys your message effectively.
  * Avoid filler words such as "umm" or "like," which can detract from your professionalism
  * Avoid passive language (i.e., "The bug was introduced by this change" -> "This change introduces a bug")
  * Speak loudly and audibly for everyone to hear clearly
2. Engage the audience
  * Maintain good eye contact
  * Avoid reading from your slides, this is an indicator that you do not know your work (and didn't practice your presentation)
  * Stand up during your presentation (but don't move around too much, since one committee member will be on zoom)
3. Presentation Slides
  * Use visuals to effectively, avoid slides with too much text and overcrowding slides with information.
4. Be Prepared for Audience Questions
5. Practice!
  * I will try to set up a reading group practice for each defense, but you should also practice on your own.
